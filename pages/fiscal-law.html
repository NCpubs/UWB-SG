Fiscal Law

Three Tenants of Fiscal Law

The foundation of federal fiscal law rests upon three fundamental tenets—Time, Purpose, and Amount—which strictly govern how and when appropriated funds can be obligated and expended.

Time

Bona Fide Need Rule

Only fulfill a genuine need that arises during the period of funds availability.

Purpose

Misappropriation Act

Only use funds only for their specified purpose.

Amount

Anti Deficiency Act

Do not spend more money than appropriated or accept unauthorized voluntary help.

Appropriation Lifecycle

An appropriation represents the legal authority granted by Congress to incur obligations and make payments out of the U.S. Treasury for specific purposes. This authority is not indefinite; it operates within a strictly defined lifecycle to ensure fiscal discipline and control over public funds.

Phase 1: Current

Appropriation is active and available for new obligations and expenditures.

The current period varies by category. For example:

Category Period (Years)
O&M (3400) 1
RDT&E (3600) 2
Procurement (multiple) 3
MILCON (3300) 5

Phase 2: Expired

No longer available for new obligations, but can be used to adjust or liquidate existing obligations.

The expired period is 5 years for all categories.

Phase 3: Cancelled

The account is closed, and any remaining balance is no longer available for any purpose.

Current funds must be secured and used for legitimate invoices.

Appropriation Category Summary

Expenses are the costs incurred to operate and maintain the organization. Investments are the costs that result in the acquisition of, or an addition to, end items that benefit future periods and generally are of a long-term character.

  • Operations and Maintenance (O&M)
    • Expenses including:
    • O&M appropriations traditionally do not finance investments, but rather those things whose benefits are derived for a limited period of time (i.e., expenses).
    • Examples of costs financed by O&M funds are headquarters operations, travel, fuel, minor construction projects less than $4M, expenses of operational military forces, training and education, recruiting, depot maintenance, purchases from Defense Working Capital Funds (e.g., spare parts), base operations support, and assets with a system unit cost less than the current expense/investment threshold ($350K).
  • Research, Development, Test and Evaluaton (RDT&E)
    • Investments and expenses, including:
    • Research, development, test and evaluation efforts.
    • End items, weapons, equipment, components, and materials as well as performance of services necessary to develop and test the system.
    • The operating costs of dedicated activities engaged in the conduct of R&D programs.
  • Procurement
    • Investments including:
    • Items whose system unit cost exceeds the current expense/investment threshold ($350K).
    • All centrally managed end items not purchased from Defense Working Capital Funds, regardless of unit cost (e.g., handguns); purchases from the Defense Working Capital Fund furnished as part of a system acquisition, system modification, major service life extension program and initial spares.
    • With certain limited exceptions, the cost of fabricating and installing additions or modifications to existing end items.
  • Military Construction (MILCON)
    • Investments including:
    • Major construction projects costing greater than $4M.
